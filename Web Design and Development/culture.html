Chinese Culture

I am Chinese. The Chinese culture is one of the world’s oldest cultures. It originated thousands of years ago.


Chinese Festivals:

In China, we celebrate Chinese new year, dragon boat day, mid-autumn festival etc.. Chinese New Year is a time to reunite with the whole family and honour our ancestors, worship gods and pray for a bumper harvest year, get rid of the old and make way for the new, participate in all kinds of creativity and have fun. Chinese new year is a holiday, often it is near the end of January. Chinese people get 7 days off work.

During Chinese New Year, people often house clean and decorate their house with the colour red, they have family reunion dinner on Chinese New Year Eve, stay up late, giving red envelopes with best wishes (often the older people give it to the younger people), set off fireworks, enjoy lion and dragon dances, the lantern festival (last day of Chinese New Year). During Chinese New Year, you should not say unlucky words, sweep the floor or take out the garbage on the first day of the New Year, break anything, take any medicine, don’t kill anything (like fish), get a haircut, lending or borrowing anything, washing clothes, hospital visits, crying or fighting as these things will give you bad luck for the rest of the year.

The lantern festival often means the end of Chinese New Year. Which means there is to be no more celebrations of the Chinese New Year until next time. People often light up lanterns, guess lanterns riddles, eat yuan xiao and enjoy time with their loved ones.

Dragon Boat Racing, eating zongzi, drinking regular wine, wearing sachets, hanging mugwort, taking a herbal bath, wear the five-coloured silk threads are some things often done during the Dragon boat festivals.

Eating moon cake, admiring the full moon, reunite with family, hang up lanterns, worship the moon, guess lantern riddles are some thing that are often done during the Mid-Autumn Festival.

The Dongzhi Festival is known as the Winter solstice or the Chinese Thanksgiving Day. It is a Chinese festival that is also celebrated. It is the shortest day of the year, and the weather, in China, will become colder and colder on this day. People celebrate the Dongzhi Festival because it is an optimistic day with a reborn and a new beginning. Chinese believes that the bad things and dark have gone away, and a better new year is coming. Dongzhi Festival is a day to for a family reunion, but it is not a public holiday yet. People often spend time with their loved ones. Traditional Chinese food like dumplings and tangyuan are commonly eaten on this day. Chinese celebrate Dongzhi Festival by worshipping their ancestors, worshipping Heaven and Earth, counting Nines (the first and second nine days, it is too cold to put your hands out of your coat. The third and fourth nine days, you can walk on ice. The fifth and sixth nine days, you will find the willows starting to sprout if you walk along the banks. In the seventh and eighth nine days, ice-out will come and wild geese will fly back to north. In the last nine days, the oxen started to work in the fields again because spring is coming.), the drink rice wine, make tofu, make cured meat for the Lunar New Year.


Zodiac Signs:

One thing that the Chinese are famous for is their zodiac system. The zodiac system consists of 12 animals that represent different years. The animal represents the person’s birth year. For example, if you were born in 2023, you would be the rabbit.


Dragon Meaning:

Another thing, that the Chinese are famous for is their dragon dance. But have you ever thought about what the Chinese dragon actually means? Chinese dragons are a symbol of strength, good luck, and power. The dragon statues/figures are everywhere in China. The Chinese dragon has five different colours. The colours of the Chinese dragon are green, white, black, red, and yellow. Each colour has a significant meaning. The dragon in the dragon dance can range in length from 9 meters to 20 meters, with at least ten men to hold the poles that raise and lower the dragon. The dragon dance is usually performed during Chinese New Year, Lantern Festival and Dragon Boat Festival.


Tea:

In China, tea is often being drink. China is the homeland of tea, it is the main producer and consumer. Tea developed from the earliest fresh-boiled tea taken as a kind of soup, to later dried and preserved teas. There are six major types of tea in China. Green tea, black tea, oolong tea, dark tea, white tea, and reprocessed tea.


Lucky and Unlucky numbers:

In China, there are lucky and unlucky numbers. The lucky numbers are 8, 9, 6, and 2. While, the unlucky numbers are 4 and 7. 1, 3, 5 are all neutral/safe numbers.

2 is a lucky number because 2 means good things come in pairs. 6 is a lucky number because 6 means everything will go smoothly. 8 is a lucky number because 8 means to make a fortune. 9 is a lucky number because 9 means long-lasting. 8 is considered the luckiest number in the Chinese culture. Lucky number combinations are 168, 520, 666 and 888.

4 is an unlucky number because 4 in Chinese sounds like a word for “death”. 7 is an unlucky number because 7 relates to the ceremonies that release dead souls.


Celebrating Two Birthdays:

Chinese people often have two people. They celebrate their Lunar Calendar birthday and their normal birthdays.

New Zealand Culture

I am a New Zealander as I am born here. New Zealand’s culture is a mix of Western and Indigenous influences.


Colonisation:

The Māori people have lived on the island since the 14th century, and the British colonisers came here in the 18th century, forever changing the course of the history for the inhabitants of New Zealand that were here before them.


Beliefs/Religions:

Most people who live in New Zealand are either Christian or not religious at all, however our country allows us to include people who believe in different faiths, like Buddhism.


Islands:

New Zealand is surrounded all by water. We have three main islands, North Island, South Island, and Stewart Island.

The North Island is the second largest island in New Zealand. The North Island is famous for volcanoes, sub-tropical beaches, big cities, and strong Māori culture. There are many things to do in the North Island.

The South Island is the largest island in New Zealand. The South Island is famous for its natural beauty, and places for winter sports (like skiing, snowboarding etc.). Many people who live in the North Island, come down to the South Island during the Winter season to enjoy the snow. This is because the North Island does not have snow, but the South Island has snow in the Winter.

Stewart Island is the third largest island in New Zealand. Stewart island is one of the best places to see the “glowing skies” or also known as the Southern Lights or Aurora Australis. Steward Island is made up of beaches, wetlands, and native forest, which makes it one of the best places to see native New Zealand wildlife in the wild. There are more kiwis (bird) that live in Stewart Island, then there is of humans. It also has one of New Zealand’s Great Walks (the Rakiura Track).


Sports:

Rugby is the most popular sport in New Zealand, but many other sports are played in New Zealand as well. Cricket, football, netball, golf, basketball, hockey, tennis, and water sports like sailing and surfing, are quite popular sports in New Zealand as well.


Dance/Music:

The traditional Māori haka is performed before any rugby match that New Zealand plays in. This is a cultural thing we do here in New Zealand.

The traditional Māori music, dance, and art forms are still practiced today, and have greatly impacted New Zealand’s artistic expression.


Food/Cuisine:

The food that the Māori people eat include food like sweet potatoes, fern roots, birds, and fish. New Zealand is famous for the seafood produced. New Zealand exports much seafood to different countries.

A hangi is a special Māori way of cooking food. It is prepared underground, using heated rocks that are buried in a pit oven. The food is then placed on top of the stones. The items are then covered with flax mats or hessian bags for three hours.

New Zealand is famous for its dairies because of the fresh cream in ice cream etc..

Special Culture/Traditions:

The hongi is a personal, customary greeting, usually saved up for special occasions. It is the pressing of noses and the foreheads; this is physical Māori greeting.
